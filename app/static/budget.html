Step 2 — Budget

💡 New Grad Budget Reality Checks
  • 🎯If rent alone is >30% of take-home, you're "rent burdened" — roommates cut this fast.
  • 🎯Contribute at minimum to your 401k employer match — that's an instant 50–100% return.
  • 🎯Student loan grace periods end 6 months after graduation. Plan for that payment now.
  • 🎯Build $1,000 in emergency savings before paying extra on loans — this prevents credit card debt when the unexpected hits.

🧾
Budget for Move-In Costs
Most landlords require first month + last month + security deposit (often = 1 month) upfront. That's 3× rent in cash. Start saving now.
🛡️
Get Renters Insurance
Typically $10–20/month. Covers your belongings if stolen, damaged, or destroyed. Many landlords require it. Get quotes on Lemonade or Policygenius.
📍
Location vs. Commute Cost
A cheaper apartment 10 miles out can cost more in gas, transit, or time than the rent savings. Factor commute into your true monthly cost.
🤝
Negotiate Everything
Ask for 1–2 months free, free parking, or a lower rate for a longer lease. The worst answer is no. First-time renters often don't ask — and leave money behind.
👤
Build Credit for Approval
Landlords run credit checks. A thin credit file = harder approval. Get a secured card or become an authorized user on a parent's card to build history fast.
📋
Read the Lease
Check: lease-break fees, subletting rules, guest policies, pet deposits, and utility responsibility. Understand everything before you sign.

💡
Grace Period Strategy
Federal loans give 6 months after graduation before payments start. Unsubsidized loans still accrue interest during this time. If possible, pay at least the interest during the grace period to prevent capitalization — where interest gets added to your principal.

First Job Checklist

What to do in your first 90 days

These steps can be worth tens of thousands of dollars over your career. Most new grads skip at least half of them. Check them off as you complete them.

0 of 10 completed
✓

Set up Direct Deposit

Bring a voided check or your routing + account number to HR on day 1. Some employers offer small bonuses for early enrollment.

✓

Complete Your W-4

Use the IRS Withholding Estimator to set it correctly and avoid a big surprise bill next April.

✓

Enroll in Health Insurance

You typically have 30 days from hire. Compare plans by total cost (premium + deductible + out-of-pocket max), not just monthly premium.

✓

Sign Up for Your 401k

Contribute at least enough to capture the full employer match. That's an instant 50–100% return. Invest in a low-cost target-date fund if unsure.

✓

Review All Benefits

FSA, HSA, dental, vision, life insurance, disability, tuition reimbursement, commuter benefits. Read everything — some benefits expire if not elected at hire.

✓

Open a Roth IRA

If income-eligible, contribute up to $7,000/year (2025). Tax-free growth — the best time to start is your first year of income.

✓

Log Into Your Loan Servicer

Know your exact balance, interest rates, and repayment start date. Explore IDR enrollment if your payment would exceed 10% of monthly income.

✓

Open a High-Yield Savings Account

Move your emergency fund to an HYSA earning 4–5% APY. Takes 5 minutes. Ally, Marcus, or Synchrony are solid options.

✓

Set Up a Budget App

YNAB, PocketGuard, or Monarch Money — link your accounts and categorize your first 30 days of spending to spot problem areas.

✓

Check Your Credit Score

Credit Karma and Experian are free. Your score matters for apartments, car loans, and even some job applications. Know where you stand.

🎯
The Priority Order (if you can't do everything at once)
  1. Capture full 401k employer match
  2. Build $1,000 starter emergency fund
  3. Pay off any high-interest debt (>8% APR)
  4. Grow emergency fund to 3 months of expenses
  5. Max out Roth IRA ($7,000/year)
  6. Extra loan payments or increase investment contributions
